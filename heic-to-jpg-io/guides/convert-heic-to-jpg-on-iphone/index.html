How to convert HEIC to JPG on iPhone or iPad

Create compatible JPG copies in Safari, understand when iOS converts photos automatically, or change the camera format for new photos.

Updated July 20267 minute readSafari-friendly

Convert one photo or a batch in Safari

You do not need to move HEIC photos to a computer first. The browser converter works on a modern iPhone or iPad and processes images on the device. This is useful when a form specifically requires .jpg, when a recipient cannot open HEIC, or when you need a batch in one ZIP.

Fastest method
  1. Open the HEIC to JPG converter in Safari.
  2. Tap the file area and choose the source offered by iOS, such as Photo Library or Files.
  3. Select one or more HEIC images.
  4. Leave quality at 80 for a balanced result, or raise it when detail matters more than file size.
  5. Download the JPG. For several photos, wait for the batch to finish and download the ZIP.

Large phone photos use much more memory while decoded than their compressed file sizes suggest. If Safari reloads the tab, retry with fewer images. The converter processes files one at a time specifically to reduce this pressure.

Why the iPhone uses HEIC

Apple adopted HEIF for photos because it offers better compression than JPEG while preserving similar visual quality. That means more images can fit on a device or in a cloud library. HEIC is a common filename extension for images stored in the HEIF format.

Apple’s current support guidance recommends the efficient formats for normal use, but it also recognizes that older formats are more broadly compatible. Whether you should change the camera setting depends on your workflow. If you mostly use Apple devices and modern photo software, HEIC is usually the better source format. If you constantly upload to a system that only accepts JPG, capturing compatible files may save time.

Make future iPhone photos use JPEG

Apple provides a camera setting for broader compatibility. On a supported iPhone or iPad:

  1. Open Settings.
  2. Tap Camera.
  3. Tap Formats.
  4. Select Most Compatible.

New photos will then use JPEG and new videos use a more compatible format. This does not convert HEIC photos that already exist in the library. It can also use more storage than the High Efficiency setting.

Official Apple reference

Apple documents both High Efficiency and Most Compatible, sharing behavior, and transfer settings in Using HEIF or HEVC media on Apple devices.

When sharing creates a compatible copy automatically

Messages, email, AirDrop, and other sharing paths may send a more compatible version when the receiving device does not support the newer format. That behavior is convenient, but it is not a dependable replacement for explicit conversion. The destination application, selected sharing method, and receiving device can all affect the result.

If the recipient must receive a file whose name ends in .jpg, convert it first and verify the downloaded filename. This avoids assuming that a sharing app changed the format.

Transferring to Windows: Automatic or Keep Originals

Apple’s Photos transfer setting influences what may happen during a USB import. Current Apple guidance places the option under Settings, Apps, Photos. A setting that favors automatic compatibility can result in JPEG copies during some transfers, while Keep Originals preserves the source HEIC. Menu locations can change between system releases, so use Apple’s current support page if the wording on your device differs.

Choose Keep Originals when you want an archive that retains the storage-efficient source. Choose a compatibility-oriented transfer when the destination cannot work with HEIC. You can also keep the originals and convert only the images that need JPG.

Quality, file size, and repeated conversion

HEIC-to-JPG conversion creates a new lossy image. At a sensible quality setting, the visual difference is often difficult to notice at normal viewing sizes, but the process is not mathematically lossless. Avoid converting a JPG back and forth between formats or repeatedly saving it after edits. Each lossy encoding can discard additional information.

For email and application forms, 75 to 85 is often enough. For an important photo that may be printed or edited, try 88 to 92 and compare the result. The resulting JPG may be larger than the original HEIC because JPEG compression is less efficient.

Protecting location metadata

A photo can contain EXIF metadata such as date, device information, and location. This data can be valuable in a private library and undesirable in a public upload. Disable Keep EXIF when privacy matters more than preserving capture details. When it is enabled, the converter attempts to retain compatible metadata, but preservation is not guaranteed for every HEIC file.

Common iPhone conversion problems

The downloaded JPG does not appear in Photos

Safari downloads typically appear in the Downloads location in Files. Open the downloaded image and use the share menu if you want to save a copy into Photos.

Safari reloads while converting

Close unused tabs and reduce the batch size. Very high-resolution images require large temporary pixel buffers even when the HEIC files look small in storage.

The file picker returns a different format

Some Apple sharing and selection workflows provide a compatible copy automatically. Inspect the filename and output before assuming the source is still HEIC.

Best long-term setup

Keep High Efficiency if storage matters and your normal devices understand HEIC. Use the browser converter for occasional compatibility copies. Switch to Most Compatible if your daily work repeatedly depends on JPG-only websites or programs. Neither choice is universally correct; the better setting is the one that minimizes conversion without giving up the source quality and storage benefits you value.
