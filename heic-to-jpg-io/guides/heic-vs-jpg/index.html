HEIC vs JPG: which format should you use?

HEIC is efficient and feature-rich; JPG is universally compatible. The right choice depends on whether you are storing a source photo or delivering a file to someone else.

Updated July 20269 minute readPractical comparison

The short answer

Keep HEIC when you are storing original iPhone or iPad photos in a modern library and want efficient files. Use JPG when the image must open, upload, print, or display reliably across the widest range of websites, devices, and software.

These are not mutually exclusive choices. A strong workflow keeps the HEIC as the source and creates a JPG copy for delivery. That preserves the efficient original while avoiding compatibility problems at the destination.

What HEIC and HEIF mean

HEIF stands for High Efficiency Image File Format. It is a container format that can store images, sequences, thumbnails, metadata, and related information. HEIC is the filename extension commonly used for HEIF images captured by Apple devices. The terms are often used interchangeably in everyday conversation, although HEIF describes the broader format.

Apple introduced HEIF capture on compatible devices alongside iOS 11 and macOS High Sierra. Apple’s support documentation explains that HEIF offers better compression than JPEG while preserving comparable visual quality. This storage advantage is the main reason an iPhone can use HEIC by default.

Primary source

Apple describes the format, compatible capture devices, sharing behavior, conversion, and the Most Compatible camera setting in Using HEIF or HEVC media on Apple devices.

Image quality: HEIC is usually more efficient

Both HEIC and JPG commonly use lossy compression for photographs, but they use different technologies. HEIC can preserve a similar-looking image at a smaller file size, or more detail at a comparable size. Exact results depend on the encoder, source image, quality setting, color information, and content.

A blanket claim such as “HEIC is always half the size” is too simple. A smooth portrait, detailed landscape, screenshot, and low-light photo compress differently. The important practical point is that HEIC was designed later and is generally more storage-efficient.

Converting HEIC to JPG requires another lossy encode. A sensible JPEG quality setting can make the visual change difficult to notice at normal viewing size, but the conversion is not lossless. Keep the source when future editing or archival quality matters.

Compatibility: JPG wins decisively

JPEG has been part of digital photography and the web for decades. Browsers, content management systems, office applications, printers, social services, and forms almost universally understand it. That broad support is why converting solves so many practical problems.

HEIC support is uneven. Current Apple systems work with it naturally, and other platforms can add or include support, but a specific app may still reject the format. Even when a computer can preview HEIC, an upload field may allow only .jpg, .jpeg, or .png.

File size: why the JPG can be larger

People often expect conversion to make a file smaller. HEIC to JPG can do the opposite. The source uses more efficient compression, and a high JPEG quality setting needs more bytes to preserve a similar appearance. A larger JPG is therefore normal.

If the destination imposes a size limit, adjust JPEG quality. Start around 82, inspect the result, and reduce gradually. Resizing image dimensions can save far more space than lowering quality, although this focused converter preserves the original dimensions.

Color, HDR, and editing

HEIF containers can carry modern color and dynamic-range information that a standard browser-created JPEG may not preserve. A simple conversion aims for a compatible visual result, not an archival replacement for every property in the source container. Professional HDR, wide-gamut, or deep editing workflows should keep the original and use color-managed software designed for that work.

JPEG remains practical for finished delivery. It is easy to preview, place in documents, upload to a CMS, send to a printer, or hand to a client. Use it as an endpoint rather than repeatedly reopening and resaving it during editing.

Metadata and privacy

Both formats can carry metadata. Camera settings and capture dates help organize a library, while GPS coordinates can reveal where a photo was taken. Conversion is an opportunity to make an explicit privacy decision.

The converter’s Keep EXIF option attempts to copy compatible metadata into the JPG. Because HEIC containers vary, preservation is best effort. Disable it before public sharing when location privacy matters, and inspect critical metadata with a dedicated viewer if the information must be retained.

Which format is better for websites?

Between HEIC and JPG, use JPG for direct website compatibility. Most browsers can display JPEG without special code, and common image pipelines know how to resize and optimize it. HEIC is a poor default delivery format for a public webpage because support is not universal.

That does not mean JPEG is always the smallest web format. WebP and AVIF may produce smaller files in a modern image pipeline. However, a CMS or upload form that asks specifically for JPG is signaling that compatibility matters more than adopting the newest format.

Which format is better for email and messaging?

JPG is the safer attachment when you cannot control the recipient’s device. Some Apple sharing methods may create compatible copies automatically, but an explicit JPG removes uncertainty. Reduce JPEG quality if an attachment limit is the concern.

Which format is better for storage?

HEIC is often better for a modern personal photo library because it preserves the efficient camera source. Storage is only one part of an archive, though. An archive also needs reliable software, backups, and a migration plan. Keep redundant backups and avoid assuming one cloud library is a permanent archive.

When should you convert HEIC to JPG?

  • A website or form rejects .heic files.
  • A recipient cannot preview the image.
  • You need to place the photo in a document or older application.
  • A print or delivery workflow specifically asks for JPEG.
  • You want a universally viewable copy alongside the original.
